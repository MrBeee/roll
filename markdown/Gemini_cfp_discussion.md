#### Please explain the exact difference between how traditional CDP fold maps footprints versus CFP focal beams

23 May 2026
The core difference lies in how they measure resolution: traditional CDP fold is a purely geometric shortcut that counts surface positions, while CFP focal beams simulate physical wave propagation to predict actual subsurface imaging quality.
Here is the exact breakdown of how they map acquisition footprints differently.

1. Traditional CDP Fold
  Common Midpoint (CDP) fold maps footprints using simple, 2D geometric sorting. It assumes the subsurface is flat and reflective. 

  * The Metric: It counts the number of seismic traces that share the exact same surface midpoint between a source and a receiver.
  * The Assumption: High fold equals good data quality; uniform fold distribution equals a footprint-free image.
  * How it Maps Footprints: It reveals footprints purely as periodic variations in surface trace density (striations or grid patterns).
  * The Fatal Flaw: It completely ignores the earth's velocity structure, wave physics, and frequency. Two surveys can have identical, perfectly uniform CDP fold maps, but one will produce a terrible, footprint-ridden subsurface image due to shallow velocity anomalies. 

2. CFP Focal Beams
  The Common Focal Point (CFP) focal-beam method maps footprints by simulating how wave energy actually travels through a complex earth model.

  * The Metric: It calculates the resolution and amplitude at a specific target point in the subsurface, rather than a point on the surface.
  * The Process: It uses "double focusing." First, it synthesizes a virtual source at the subsurface target point and propagates energy upward to the actual surface source positions (Source Beam). Second, it does the same for the receiver positions (Receiver Beam).
  * How it Maps Footprints: By multiplying the source and receiver beams together, it creates a focal-beam matrix. This matrix directly visualizes spatial variations in amplitude, phase, and resolution at the reservoir level.
  * The Major Advantage: It honors wave theory, aperture limitations, and complex velocities. It shows you the actual footprint—such as artificial amplitude striping caused by acquisition geometry interacting with a complex overburden (like gas clouds or salt layers)—before you ever shoot the survey. 

  ##### Summary Comparison

  | Feature         | Traditional CDP Fold             | CFP Focal Beams                                 |
  | --------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
  | Domain          | Surface grid geometry            | Subsurface target location                      |
  | Physics         | Straight lines (No physics)      | Full wave propagation & frequency               |
  | Overburden      | Assumed flat and uniform         | Handles complex layers and velocity changes     |
  | Footprint Cause | Traces missing from surface bins | Uneven energy illumination at the target        |
  | Output Map      | Red/blue surface density grid    | A realistic subsurface amplitude/resolution map |

  ##### How it alters the Focal Beam:

  * Narrow Aspect Ratios (e.g., 0.2 to 0.4): Typical of cheaper marine towed-streamer surveys or narrow land geometries. The focal beam becomes highly elliptical at the target. You achieve sharp, high-resolution imaging in the in-line direction, but a smeared, low-resolution beam in the cross-line direction. This creates a severe directional acquisition footprint.
  * Wide Aspect Ratios (e.g., 0.8 to 1.0): Typical of Ocean Bottom Node (OBN) or high-density land surveys. The source and receiver beams intersect symmetrically. The resulting focal beam is circular, meaning spatial resolution is equal in all horizontal directions.
  * The Depth Factor: Focal beams change with depth. A narrow surface geometry actually acts like a wider geometry for very deep targets because the angular aperture changes. CFP focal beams map this exact transition, telling you precisely at what depth a narrow survey's footprint disappears.

##### How they are calculated using CFP:

1. Forward Modeling: You define a subsurface target horizon and place a grid of virtual focal points across it.
2. One-Way Propagation: You propagate wavefields upward from these focal points to the surface through a velocity model. This simulates the Green’s function (the wave path).
3. Surface Filtering: The algorithm applies your proposed acquisition geometry as a spatial mask on the surface. Any simulated energy that hits an area without a real-world source or receiver is deleted.
4. Downward Synthesis: The remaining, filtered surface energy is propagated back down to the target.
5. Mask Generation: The final energy distribution at the target is normalized between 0 (complete shadow) and 1 (perfect illumination). This grid is your Illumination Mask.

##### Why this eliminates footprints:

If your illumination mask shows a stripe of low energy at the reservoir, you know that area will look dim in your final seismic image.

Traditional CDP design would assume this dimness is a geological change (like a reservoir pinching out). The CFP illumination mask proves it is just an acquisition footprint caused by the geometry interacting with the overburden, allowing you to modify shot locations before moving into the field.

import numpy as np
import numba as nb

@nb.njit(parallel=True, cache=True)
def compute_one_way_beam(focal_x, focal_y, focal_z, surf_x, surf_y, surf_z, freqs, v_int):
    """
    Computes the one-way synthesized wavefield beam at a specific subsurface 
    focal point across an array of surface coordinates and frequency bands.
    
    Parameters:
    -----------
    focal_x, focal_y, focal_z : float
        Coordinates of the target subsurface focal point.
    surf_x, surf_y, surf_z : 1D float arrays
        Coordinates of the active surface channels (either sources or receivers).
    freqs : 1D float array
        The discrete frequency spectrum to evaluate (e.g., 10 to 60 Hz).
    v_int : float
        Interval velocity (vint from your XML configuration).
        
    Returns:
    --------
    beam_spectrum : 1D complex array
        The synthesized monochromatic energy summed across the array per frequency.
    """
    n_surf = len(surf_x)
    n_freq = len(freqs)
    
    # Pre-allocate output array for each frequency channel
    beam_spectrum = np.zeros(n_freq, dtype=np.complex128)
    
    # Loop over frequencies in parallel
    for f_idx in nb.prange(n_freq):
        omega = 2.0 * np.pi * freqs[f_idx]
        k = omega / v_int  # Wavenumber
        
        total_field = 0.0 + 0.0j
        
        # Vectorized/Optimized loop over surface layout coordinates
        for s_idx in range(n_surf):
            dx = surf_x[s_idx] - focal_x
            dy = surf_y[s_idx] - focal_y
            dz = surf_z[s_idx] - focal_z # Usually 0.0 or elevation
            
            # Distance from target point to surface coordinate
            r = np.sqrt(dx*dx + dy*dy + dz*dz)
            
            if r < 1e-3:
                continue
                
            # Rayleigh-II green's function equation components
            obliquity = np.abs(dz) / r
            phase = -k * r
            amplitude = (obliquity / (2.0 * np.pi * r)) * (1.0 / r)
            
            # Complex wavefield: Amplitude * exp(j * phase) * scaling factor
            # (Simplification ignoring the near-field 1/r terms for deep targets)
            real_part = amplitude * np.cos(phase)
            imag_part = amplitude * np.sin(phase)
            
            total_field += complex(real_part, imag_part)
            
        beam_spectrum[f_idx] = total_field
        
    return beam_spectrum

import numpy as np
import numba as nb

@nb.njit(parallel=True, cache=True)
def filter_indices_by_aperture(focal_x, focal_y, focal_z, max_dip_degrees, mmap_mid_x, mmap_mid_y):
    """
    Scans the memory-mapped coordinates and isolates trace indices that fall 
    within the physical illumination cone of the specific subsurface target.
    
    Parameters:
    -----------
    focal_x, focal_y, focal_z : float
        Coordinates of the target subsurface focal point.
    max_dip_degrees : float
        The maximum structural dip angle to clear for imaging (e.g., 35.0 or 45.0).
    mmap_mid_x, mmap_mid_y : 1D float arrays (Memory-Mapped)
        The horizontal surface midpoints for ALL traces in the survey design file.
        
    Returns:
    --------
    valid_indices : 1D int64 array
        The filtered subset of indices to pass to your extraction engine.
    """
    n_traces = len(mmap_mid_x)
    
    # Calculate the maximum allowable horizontal distance on the surface
    theta_rad = np.radians(max_dip_degrees)
    max_radius = np.abs(focal_z) * np.tan(theta_rad)
    max_radius_sq = max_radius * max_radius
    
    # Step 1: Pre-screen flag array to allow parallel execution without race conditions
    keep_mask = np.zeros(n_traces, dtype=nb.boolean)
    
    for i in nb.prange(n_traces):
        dx = mmap_mid_x[i] - focal_x
        dy = mmap_mid_y[i] - focal_y
        
        # Euclidean distance squared (avoids calculating expensive square roots)
        distance_sq = dx*dx + dy*dy
        
        if distance_sq <= max_radius_sq:
            keep_mask[i] = True
            
    # Step 2: Extract active index locations
    # (Using non-parallel standard loop to cleanly compress the index mask)
    valid_indices = np.where(keep_mask)[0]
    
    return valid_indices

import numpy as np
import numba as nb

@nb.njit(parallel=True, cache=True)
def filter_sps_relations_by_aperture(focal_x, focal_y, max_radius, src_x, src_y, rec_x, rec_y):
    """
    Screens memory-mapped active relation arrays to isolate traces where 
    BOTH source and receiver contribute to the CFP focal point.
    """
    n_traces = len(src_x)
    keep_mask = np.zeros(n_traces, dtype=nb.boolean)
    r_sq = max_radius * max_radius

    for i in nb.prange(n_traces):
        # 1. Evaluate Source Proximity
        dsx = src_x[i] - focal_x
        dsy = src_y[i] - focal_y
        if (dsx*dsx + dsy*dsy) > r_sq:
            continue  # Source is outside aperture
            
        # 2. Evaluate Receiver Proximity
        drx = rec_x[i] - focal_x
        dry = rec_y[i] - focal_y
        if (drx*drx + dry*dry) > r_sq:
            continue  # Receiver is outside aperture
            
        keep_mask[i] = True
    
    return np.where(keep_mask)[0]

@nb.njit(parallel=True, cache=True)
def compute_monochromatic_beam(focal_x, focal_y, focal_z, surf_x, surf_y, freq, v_int):
    """
    Calculates a single frequency wavefront component for the isolated target channels.
    """
    n_stations = len(surf_x)
    omega = 2.0 * np.pi * freq
    k = omega / v_int
   
    # Store complex components as complex128 array
    wavefield = np.zeros(n_stations, dtype=np.complex128)
   
    for i in nb.prange(n_stations):
        dx = surf_x[i] - focal_x
        dy = surf_y[i] - focal_y
        r = np.sqrt(dx*dx + dy*dy + focal_z*focal_z)
        
        if r < 1e-3:
            continue
            
        # Rayleigh-II obliquity factor and green's function wave propagation
        obliquity = np.abs(focal_z) / r
        phase = -k * r
        amplitude = obliquity / (2.0 * np.pi * r * r)
        
        wavefield[i] = complex(amplitude * np.cos(phase), amplitude * np.sin(phase))
   
    return wavefield
